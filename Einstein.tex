\begin{abstract}
We prove that for static spherically symmetric spacetimes
$ds^2 = -f\,dt^2 + f^{-1}dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2$
with symplectic eigenvalues $\sigma_1 = r\sqrt{f}$ and $\sigma_2 = r$,
the vacuum Einstein equations are equivalent to a single
\emph{eigenvalue self-consistency principle}:
\begin{equation*}
  \sigma_2^2\;\Box\ln\sigma_i = 1
  \quad\text{for each eigenvalue } \sigma_i
  \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad R_{\mu\nu} = 0\,.
\end{equation*}
The $\sigma_1$ instance gives the Ricci scalar condition $R=0$,
admitting the two-parameter family $f = 1 - C_1/r - C_2/r^2$.
The $\sigma_2$ instance gives the angular condition $R_{\theta\theta}=0$,
selecting Schwarzschild uniquely.
In the weak field, the $\sigma_2$ condition yields the Newtonian
potential $\varphi = C/r$ in one line.
Both conditions are instances of a single principle:
$\Box\ln\sigma = 1/\sigma_2^2$ for each eigenvalue.
This extends the point-level $K{=}1$ self-consistency of
Chronogeometrodynamics to the field level.
We discuss the cost interpretation of matter, the cosmological
constant, the parallel with Jacobson's thermodynamic derivation,
and the prospect of singularity resolution.
\end{abstract}

%===================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%===================================================================

Several derivations of Einstein's field equations are known beyond
Einstein's original geometric argument~\cite{Einstein1915} and
Hilbert's variational principle~\cite{Hilbert1915}.
Jacobson~\cite{Jacobson1995} showed that applying the Clausius
relation $\delta Q = T\,\delta S$ with $S = A/4$ on every local
Rindler horizon yields $G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$.
Padmanabhan~\cite{Padmanabhan2010} and Verlinde~\cite{Verlinde2011}
proposed related thermodynamic and entropic approaches.
All assume Lorentzian signature as a starting point.

The $K{=}1$ Chronogeometrodynamics framework~\cite{Li2026math,Li2026real}
removes this assumption.
Three axioms on an information-time cost function---Realizability~(R),
Expansion~(E), and Temporal cost~(T)---fix the Hessian signature
to $(1,1)$, deriving the Lorentzian character of the metric.
On each local Rindler wedge, the $2\times 2$ cost metric
$G = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1^2,\,-1)$ has signature $(+,-)$,
and the self-consistency condition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K1}
  K \;:=\; \boldsymbol{x}^T\!G\,\boldsymbol{x}
  \;=\; \sigma_1^2\,x_0^2 - x_1^2 \;=\; 1
\end{equation}
states that temporal cost balances spatial cost.
A smooth restoring potential $V = \tfrac{1}{2}(K{-}1)^2$
drives Ornstein--Uhlenbeck dynamics whose stationary variance
reproduces the Tolman--Unruh temperature, furnishing two of
Jacobson's three inputs.
The Einstein equations then follow via the Jacobson bridge once
$S \propto A$ is imposed.

The reliance on $S \propto A$ and on the thermodynamic bridge
leaves this chain incomplete.
In this paper we establish a more direct connection for the
spherically symmetric sector.
We show that a single principle---\emph{every symplectic eigenvalue
satisfies} $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i = 1$---is algebraically
equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}).
The principle requires that each eigenvalue propagate at the
angular curvature rate---a field-level analogue of the
point-level balance $K{=}1$.
No thermodynamic input is used.
The scope and limitations---including the extension to general
metrics and the absence of new predictions---are discussed
in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

%===================================================================
\section{Symplectic eigenvalue self-consistency}
\label{sec:field}
%===================================================================

We work with the static spherically symmetric metric
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  ds^2 = -f(r)\,dt^2 + f(r)^{-1}\,dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2\,,
\end{equation}
with $f(r) > 0$ outside the horizon.
The symplectic eigenvalues are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigmas}
  \sigma_1 = r\sqrt{f}\,,\qquad \sigma_2 = r\,.
\end{equation}
We denote the scalar d'Alembertian by
$\Box\phi = (r^2 f)^{-1}\,\partial_r(r^2 f\,\partial_r\phi)$.

\begin{definition}[Eigenvalue self-consistency]\label{def:esc}
The \emph{eigenvalue self-consistency conditions} are
\begin{align}
  K_1 \;&:=\; \sigma_2^2\;\Box\ln\sigma_1 \;=\; 1\,,
  \label{eq:K1def}\\[4pt]
  K_2 \;&:=\; \sigma_2^2\;\Box\ln\sigma_2 \;=\; 1\,.
  \label{eq:K2def}
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\noindent
Both have the same form: $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma = 1$
for each eigenvalue~$\sigma$.
The physical content is that the d'Alembertian of each
log-eigenvalue equals the angular curvature cost $1/\sigma_2^2$:
every eigenvalue ``propagates'' at the rate set by the angular sector.

\subsection{Explicit formulas}

Expanding $\ln\sigma_1 = \ln r + \frac{1}{2}\ln f$,
a straightforward computation gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K1explicit}
  K_1 \;=\; f + 2r\,f' + \tfrac{r^2}{2}\,f''\,.
\end{equation}

For $\sigma_2 = r$, we have $\ln\sigma_2 = \ln r$ and
$(\ln r)' = 1/r$.
Since $\sigma_2^2 = r^2$ and
$\Box(\ln r) = (1/r^2)\,d/dr[r^2 f/r]$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K2explicit}
  K_2 \;=\; r^2 \cdot \frac{1}{r^2}\,\frac{d(rf)}{dr}
  \;=\; \frac{d(rf)}{dr}
  \;=\; r\,f' + f\,.
\end{equation}

\begin{remark}
$K_2 = rf'+f = d(rf)/dr$ has a direct geometric meaning:
it equals $1 - R_{\theta\theta}$, where $R_{\theta\theta} = 1-f-rf'$
is the angular Ricci component.
Equivalently, $K_2 = 1$ states that the Misner--Sharp mass
$m = \frac{r}{2}(1{-}f)$ is constant: $dm/dr = 0$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Connection to curvature}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:R}
For the metric~\eqref{eq:metric},
$R = -2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1 + 2/r^2$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
The Ricci scalar is
$R = -f'' - 4f'/r + 2(1{-}f)/r^2$.
From~\eqref{eq:K1explicit},
$\Box\ln\sigma_1 = K_1/r^2 = f/r^2 + 2f'/r + f''/2$.
Then $-2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1 + 2/r^2
= -2f/r^2 - 4f'/r - f'' + 2/r^2 = R$.
\end{proof}

\noindent
Lemma~\ref{lem:R} gives
$K_1 = 1 \;\Leftrightarrow\; R = 0$.
Together with $K_2 = 1 \;\Leftrightarrow\; R_{\theta\theta} = 0$,
these are the two independent vacuum equations for~\eqref{eq:metric}.

%===================================================================
\section{Equivalence theorem}
\label{sec:theorem}
%===================================================================

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
For the metric~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^2(0,\infty)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main}
  \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i = 1
  \;\;\text{for } i=1,2
  \qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad
  R_{\mu\nu} = 0\,.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The independent Ricci components for~\eqref{eq:metric} are
$R_{tt} \propto f'' + 2f'/r$ and
$R_{\theta\theta} = 1 - f - rf'$.
($R_{rr}=0$ is equivalent to $R_{tt}=0$ for this diagonal ansatz.)

\medskip\noindent
$(\Rightarrow)$\;
$K_2{=}1$ gives $rf'+f=1$, i.e.\ $R_{\theta\theta}=0$.
Subtracting:
$K_1 - K_2 = rf' + \frac{r^2}{2}f'' = 0$,
hence $f'' = -2f'/r$ and $R_{tt}=0$.

\medskip\noindent
$(\Leftarrow)$\;
$R_{\theta\theta}=0$ gives $K_2=1$.
$R_{tt}=0$ gives $f''=-2f'/r$.
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:K1explicit}:
$K_1 = f + 2rf' + \frac{r^2}{2}(-\frac{2f'}{r})
= f + rf' = 1$,
using $K_2{=}1$.
\end{proof}

%===================================================================
\section{Solution structure}
\label{sec:solutions}
%===================================================================

\subsection{General solution of $K_1=1$}

The ODE $f + 2rf' + \frac{r^2}{2}f'' = 1$
admits the ansatz $f = 1 - A\,r^{-n}$;
the coefficient $-(n{-}1)(n{-}2)/2$ vanishes for $n=1,2$.
Since the ODE is second-order and linear, this is the complete
general solution:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gensol}
  f(r) = 1 - \frac{C_1}{r} - \frac{C_2}{r^2}\,.
\end{equation}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:scalar}
$K_1{=}1$ alone is equivalent to $R=0$, strictly weaker than
$R_{\mu\nu}=0$.
The parameter $C_2$ in~\eqref{eq:gensol} measures the gap:
for~\eqref{eq:gensol}, $K_2 = 1 + C_2/r^2$, so
$K_2{=}1$ forces $C_2 = 0$, recovering
$f = 1 - 2M/r$ uniquely (Birkhoff).
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Linearized regime}

For $f = 1 + \varepsilon\,\varphi(r)$ with $|\varepsilon|\ll 1$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lin}
  K_2 = 1:\quad
  r\,\varphi' + \varphi = 0
  \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;
  \frac{d(r\varphi)}{dr} = 0
  \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\;
  \varphi = \frac{C}{r}\,.
\end{equation}
With $C = -2M$ this is the Newtonian potential.
The condition $K_1{=}1$ is automatically satisfied at linear order,
meaning $K_1$ carries independent content only at nonlinear
(strong-field) order.
Any future observational test distinguishing the eigenvalue
principle from standard GR would therefore require
strong-gravity data.

\section{Non-vacuum sector and cosmological constant}
\label{sec:nonvac}
%===================================================================

\subsection{Matter as eigenvalue imbalance}

The Einstein $G_{tt}$ component gives
$rf'+f-1 = -8\pi\rho\,r^2$, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho}
  \rho \;=\; \frac{1 - K_2}{8\pi\,r^2}
  \;=\; \frac{1 - \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_2}{8\pi\,\sigma_2^2}\,.
\end{equation}
Normal matter ($\rho > 0$) corresponds to $K_2 < 1$:
the $\sigma_2$ eigenvalue fails to propagate at the angular
curvature rate, and the deficit per unit area is the energy density.

We stress that~\eqref{eq:rho} is a \emph{rewriting} of the
Einstein equations: the coefficient $8\pi$ is inherited from GR,
not derived from the cost framework.

\subsection{Cosmological constant}

For Schwarzschild--de~Sitter, $f = 1 - 2M/r - \Lambda r^2/3$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Lambda}
  K_2 = 1 - \Lambda\,r^2\,,\qquad
  K_1 = 1 - 2\Lambda\,r^2\,.
\end{equation}
The cosmological constant shifts both eigenvalue conditions
away from unity; its value is not predicted.

\section{Connection to Jacobson's approach}
\label{sec:general}
%===================================================================

\subsection{Polarization identity}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:polar}
If $R_{\mu\nu}$ is symmetric with $R = 0$ and
$R_{\mu\nu}\,v^\mu v^\nu = 0$ for every null $v^\mu$,
then $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In an orthonormal frame, null vectors are $v = e_0 + n^i e_i$,
$|n|=1$.
Taking $n = \pm e_k$: $R_{0k}=0$, $R_{00}+R_{kk}=0$.
Taking $n = (e_i{+}e_j)/\sqrt{2}$: $R_{ij}=0$ for $i\neq j$.
Thus $R_{\mu\nu} \propto \mathrm{diag}(-1,\ldots,-1)$;
$R=0$ forces the constant of proportionality to vanish.
\end{proof}

\subsection{General metrics}

For a general metric, at each point one defines $\sigma_1(v)$ on
each null direction~$v$.
If $\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i = 1$ generalizes to every null
sector, one obtains $R=0$ and $R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu = 0$
for all null~$v$; the polarization identity then gives
$R_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

The step connecting the eigenvalue condition to
$R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu = 0$ on each null plane requires the
Gauss--Codazzi relations for null hypersurfaces and is
\emph{not yet completed}.
We note that the overall structure---a local condition on each null
direction, assembled into a tensor equation by polarization---is
the same logical architecture as Jacobson's
derivation~\cite{Jacobson1995},
with eigenvalue self-consistency replacing the Clausius relation.

\subsection{What is independent of Jacobson}

In the spherically symmetric sector, the equivalence
(Theorem~\ref{thm:main}) is proved by direct algebra,
\emph{without} any thermodynamic input:
no Clausius relation, no $S = A/4$, no horizon entropy.
Both $K_1$ and $K_2$ arise from the same
eigenvalue self-consistency principle,
and together they give $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.

The Jacobson parallel emerges only in the extension to general
metrics, where the null-direction patching logic reappears.

\section{Toward singularity resolution}
\label{sec:singularity}
%===================================================================

While the preceding analysis is entirely classical, the $K{=}1$
framework admits a natural interface with quantum theory through
the symplectic eigenvalue~$\sigma_1$.
If $K$ and the boost angle $\theta$ are canonically conjugate
(Open Question~7 of~\cite{Li2026math}),
then $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_{1,\min} > 0$ by the uncertainty principle.
This would bound curvature:
\begin{equation}
  A_{\min} = 4\pi\sigma_{1,\min}^2 \sim \pi\,\ell_P^2\,,\qquad
  R_{\max} \sim \frac{2}{\sigma_{1,\min}^2}
  \sim 3\times 10^{70}\;\mathrm{m^{-2}}\,,
\end{equation}
replacing the Schwarzschild singularity with a Planck-density core,
a factor of $\sim 10^{77}$ below the curvature at a solar-mass horizon.
Proving $\sigma_{1,\min}>0$ remains the most promising open direction.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%===================================================================

The main result is that the vacuum Einstein equations for
spherically symmetric spacetimes follow from a single principle:
$\sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_i = 1$ for each symplectic eigenvalue
(Theorem~\ref{thm:main}).
Both the radial condition ($K_1{=}1$, giving $R{=}0$) and the
angular condition ($K_2{=}1$, giving $R_{\theta\theta}{=}0$)
are instances of the same eigenvalue self-consistency,
removing the need to impose the angular equation by hand.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Spherical symmetry only.}
  The equivalence is proved for the ansatz~\eqref{eq:metric}.
  The general proof requires the null-surface Gauss--Codazzi step.

\item \textbf{Jacobson parallel.}
  The extension to general metrics shares the logical architecture
  of Jacobson's derivation: local condition $\times$ null directions
  $\to$ tensor equation.
  In the spherically symmetric sector, however, the derivation is
  independent of Jacobson and uses no thermodynamics.

\item \textbf{No new predictions.}
  The equivalence is exact: no deviation from GR is predicted in vacuum.
  The matter formula~\eqref{eq:rho} uses Einstein's equations as input.

\item \textbf{Linearized redundancy.}
  $K_1$ is automatically satisfied at linear order;
  it carries independent content only in the strong-field regime.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Open problems}
\label{sec:open}

\begin{enumerate}
\item Complete the general proof via null-surface Gauss--Codazzi.
\item Prove $\sigma_{1,\min}>0$ and establish curvature bounds.
\item Extend the eigenvalue principle to non-vacuum
  (derive $8\pi$ from cost).
\item Identify testable predictions in the strong-field regime.
\end{enumerate}
