\begin{abstract}
Within the $K{=}1$ Chronogeometrodynamics framework, the point-level
self-consistency condition $K = \boldsymbol{x}^{T}\!G\,\boldsymbol{x} = 1$
encodes that temporal information cost balances spatial displacement cost.
We promote this to the field level by introducing two observables for
static spherically symmetric spacetimes
$ds^2 = -f\,dt^2 + f^{-1}dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2$:
the \emph{radial cost} $K_{\mathrm{f}} \equiv \sigma_2^2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1
= f + 2rf' + \frac{r^2}{2}f''$
and the \emph{angular cost} $K_{\mathrm{a}} \equiv rf'+f$,
where $\sigma_1 = r\sqrt{f}$ and $\sigma_2 = r$ are the symplectic
eigenvalues and $\Box$ is the scalar d'Alembertian.
We prove:
\[
  K_{\mathrm{f}} = 1 \;\wedge\; K_{\mathrm{a}} = 1
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R_{\mu\nu} = 0\,.
\]
The condition $K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$ alone is equivalent to the vanishing
of the Ricci scalar ($R=0$) and admits the two-parameter solution family
$f = 1 - C_1/r - C_2/r^2$; the angular condition $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$
eliminates the second parameter, recovering Schwarzschild uniquely.
In the weak-field limit, $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ yields the Newtonian
potential $\varphi = C/r$ in one line.
We interpret non-vacuum configurations through
$\rho = (1 - K_{\mathrm{a}})/(8\pi r^2)$,
identify the cosmological constant as a target shift
$K = 1 \to 1 - \Lambda r^2$,
and discuss the relationship to Jacobson's thermodynamic derivation.
All algebraic results are verified symbolically.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%===================================================================

Five routes to Einstein's field equations are known.
Einstein's original geometric reasoning~\cite{Einstein1915} and
Hilbert's variational principle~\cite{Hilbert1915} date to 1915.
Jacobson~\cite{Jacobson1995} showed in 1995 that applying the Clausius
relation $\delta Q = T\,\delta S$ together with $S = A/4$ on every
local Rindler horizon yields $G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$.
Padmanabhan~\cite{Padmanabhan2010} reformulated the equations as
holographic equipartition, and Verlinde~\cite{Verlinde2011}
derived Newton's law from an entropic force.
All five routes assume Lorentzian signature as input.

The $K{=}1$ Chronogeometrodynamics program~\cite{Li2026math,Li2026real}
removes this assumption: three axioms on an information-time cost
function---Realizability~(R), Expansion~(E), and Temporal cost~(T)---fix
the metric signature to be $(1,1)$ on every local Rindler wedge.
The self-consistency condition $K = \boldsymbol{x}^{T}\!G\,\boldsymbol{x} = 1$
then generates Ornstein--Uhlenbeck dynamics whose stationary
distribution reproduces the Unruh temperature, providing two of
Jacobson's three inputs.
Einstein's equations follow via the Jacobson bridge once $S \propto A$ is
imposed.

A natural question remains: is the thermodynamic bridge necessary,
or can one pass from cost self-consistency to curvature more directly?

In this paper we make the passage explicit for the spherically
symmetric sector.
We define two field-level cost observables---$K_{\mathrm{f}}$
(radial--temporal) and $K_{\mathrm{a}}$ (angular)---and prove
that their simultaneous vanishing $K_{\mathrm{f}} = K_{\mathrm{a}} = 1$
is algebraically equivalent to $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$.
We emphasize from the outset that the angular observable $K_{\mathrm{a}}$
is \emph{defined} to coincide with the standard GR condition
$R_{\theta\theta}=0$; we do not derive from cost axioms why it should
equal unity.
The general-metric extension requires Jacobson-style null-direction
patching and is not completed here.
The present work is therefore a cost-based \emph{reinterpretation}
of the vacuum Einstein equations, not an independent derivation---but
one that furnishes concrete new observables and a transparent
point-to-field correspondence.

\section{Preliminaries: point-level $K{=}1$}
\label{sec:point}
%===================================================================

We briefly recall the essentials from~\cite{Li2026math}.
At each point of a Rindler wedge the cost metric is the
$2\times 2$ matrix $G = \mathrm{diag}(-\sigma_1^2,\,1)$
in boost--spatial coordinates, with $\sigma_1$ the symplectic
eigenvalue encoding the local acceleration scale.
The condition
\begin{equation}\label{eq:K1}
  K \;=\; \sigma_1^2\,x_0^2 - x_1^2 \;=\; 1
\end{equation}
defines a one-sheeted hyperboloid: temporal cost equals spatial cost.
A smooth restoring potential $V = \tfrac{1}{2}(K-1)^2$ drives
fluctuations back to $K=1$; the resulting OU process has stationary
variance $\mathrm{Var}(K) = T_{\mathrm{eff}}$, identified with the
Tolman temperature.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Question.}
Can the \emph{field-level} analogue of $K=1$ be formulated,
and does it reproduce Einstein's vacuum equations?

\section{Field-level cost observables}
\label{sec:field}
%===================================================================

Throughout this section we work with the static spherically
symmetric metric
\begin{equation}\label{eq:metric}
  ds^2 = -f(r)\,dt^2 + f(r)^{-1}\,dr^2 + r^2\,d\Omega^2\,,
\end{equation}
where $f(r) > 0$ outside the horizon.
The symplectic eigenvalues are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigmas}
  \sigma_1 = r\sqrt{f}\,,\qquad \sigma_2 = r\,.
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Radial cost]\label{def:Kf}
Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kf}
  K_{\mathrm{f}} \;:=\; \sigma_2^2\;\Box\ln\sigma_1\,,
\end{equation}
where $\Box\phi = (r^2 f)^{-1}\,\partial_r\!\bigl(r^2 f\,\partial_r\phi\bigr)$
is the scalar d'Alembertian of~\eqref{eq:metric}.
\end{definition}

An explicit computation gives
\begin{align}
  \ln\sigma_1 &= \ln r + \tfrac{1}{2}\ln f\,,\nonumber\\
  r^2 f\,(\ln\sigma_1)' &= r\,f + \tfrac{r^2}{2}\,f' \;=\; r-M
  \quad\text{(for Schwarzschild)}\,,\nonumber\\
  \frac{d}{dr}\!\bigl[r^2 f\,(\ln\sigma_1)'\bigr] &= K_{\mathrm{f}}\,,
  \label{eq:Kf-expand}
\end{align}
yielding
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Kf-explicit}
  \boxed{\;K_{\mathrm{f}} \;=\; f + 2r\,f' + \tfrac{r^2}{2}\,f''\;\;}
\end{equation}
The condition $K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$ is equivalent to $\Box\ln\sigma_1 = 1/r^2$,
which, via the identity $R = -2\,\Box\ln\sigma_1 + 2/r^2$ established
in~\cite{Li2026symp}, is equivalent to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:R0}
  R = 0\,.
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Angular cost]\label{def:Ka}
Define
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Ka}
  \boxed{\;K_{\mathrm{a}} \;:=\; r\,f' + f\;\;}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:Ka}
The observable $K_{\mathrm{f}}$ has a direct cost origin:
it measures whether the radial variation of $\sigma_1$
(information-time eigenvalue) balances the angular curvature cost
$1/\sigma_2^2 = 1/r^2$.
In contrast, $K_{\mathrm{a}} = rf'+f = d(rf)/dr$ is the derivative
of the Misner--Sharp mass function, and $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ is simply
$R_{\theta\theta}=0$.
We \emph{define} $K_{\mathrm{a}}$ this way to complete the equivalence;
an independent cost derivation of the angular condition is an open problem.
\end{remark}

\section{Main result: equivalence theorem}
\label{sec:theorem}
%===================================================================

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:main}
For the metric~\eqref{eq:metric} with $f\in C^2(0,\infty)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main}
  K_{\mathrm{f}} = 1 \;\wedge\; K_{\mathrm{a}} = 1
  \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
  R_{\mu\nu} = 0\,.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The standard Ricci components for~\eqref{eq:metric} are:
\begin{align}
  R_{tt} &\propto f'' + \frac{2f'}{r}\,,\label{eq:Rtt}\\[2pt]
  R_{\theta\theta} &= 1 - f - rf'\,.\label{eq:Rthth}
\end{align}
(Here $R_{rr}=0$ is equivalent to $R_{tt}=0$ for diagonal metrics.)

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Forward ($\Rightarrow$).}
Equation~\eqref{eq:Ka} with $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ gives $rf'+f=1$,
hence $R_{\theta\theta}=0$ by~\eqref{eq:Rthth}.
Subtracting $K_{\mathrm{a}}$ from $K_{\mathrm{f}}$:
\[
  K_{\mathrm{f}} - K_{\mathrm{a}}
  = r\,f' + \tfrac{r^2}{2}\,f'' = 0
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  f'' = -\frac{2f'}{r}\,,
\]
so $R_{tt}=0$ by~\eqref{eq:Rtt}.
Together: all $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.

\medskip\noindent
\textbf{Reverse ($\Leftarrow$).}
$R_{\theta\theta}=0$ gives $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ directly from~\eqref{eq:Rthth}.
From $R_{tt}=0$: $f'' = -2f'/r$.
Substituting into~\eqref{eq:Kf-explicit}:
\[
  K_{\mathrm{f}}
  = f + 2rf' + \tfrac{r^2}{2}\Bigl(-\frac{2f'}{r}\Bigr)
  = f + 2rf' - rf'
  = f + rf' = 1\,,
\]
using $R_{\theta\theta}=0$ in the last step.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:Kf-alone}
$K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$ alone is equivalent to $R=0$, which is strictly
weaker than $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.
The angular condition provides the additional constraint
$R_{\theta\theta}=0$ that promotes the scalar equation to a
tensor equation.
\end{corollary}

\section{Solution structure}
\label{sec:solutions}
%===================================================================

\subsection{General solution of $K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$}

The ODE~\eqref{eq:Kf-explicit} with $K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ode}
  f + 2r\,f' + \tfrac{r^2}{2}\,f'' = 1\,,
\end{equation}
admits a power-law analysis.
Setting $f = 1 - A\,r^{-n}$, the left-hand side evaluates to
$1 - \frac{(n-1)(n-2)}{2}\,A\,r^{-n}$, which equals~1 when
$(n-1)(n-2) = 0$, i.e., $n=1$ or $n=2$.
The general solution is therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gensol}
  f(r) = 1 - \frac{C_1}{r} - \frac{C_2}{r^2}\,.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Effect of $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$}

For~\eqref{eq:gensol}, $K_{\mathrm{a}} = rf'+f = 1 + C_2/r^2$.
Hence $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ forces $C_2 = 0$, and with $C_1 = 2M$
one recovers the Schwarzschild metric uniquely---in agreement
with Birkhoff's theorem.

\subsection{Linearized limit}

Writing $f = 1 + \varepsilon\,\varphi(r)$ with $|\varepsilon|\ll 1$,
the condition $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ reduces to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lin}
  r\,\varphi' + \varphi = 0
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  \frac{d}{dr}(r\varphi) = 0
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  \varphi = \frac{C}{r}\,.
\end{equation}
With $C = -2M$ this is the Newtonian gravitational potential.
Notably, $K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$ is \emph{automatically} satisfied at linear
order: the radial cost observable is redundant in the weak-field regime
and becomes an independent constraint only at nonlinear order.

\section{Non-vacuum sector and cosmological constant}
\label{sec:nonvac}
%===================================================================

\subsection{Matter as cost imbalance}

The Einstein $G_{tt}$ component reads $(1{-}f)/r^2 - f'/r = 8\pi\rho$,
which rearranges to $rf'+f-1 = -8\pi\rho\,r^2$, giving
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rho}
  \rho = \frac{1 - K_{\mathrm{a}}}{8\pi\,r^2}\,.
\end{equation}
The energy density is the angular cost deficit per unit area:
$K_{\mathrm{a}} < 1$ corresponds to positive energy.
This relation has been verified symbolically for both uniform
($\rho = \rho_0$) and non-uniform ($\rho = \rho_0(1{-}r/a)$)
density profiles.

We emphasize that~\eqref{eq:rho} is a \emph{rewriting} of the
Einstein equations in cost language, not a derivation from cost axioms.
In particular, the coefficient $8\pi$ is inherited from GR.

\subsection{Cosmological constant}

For the Schwarzschild--de~Sitter metric
$f = 1 - 2M/r - \Lambda r^2/3$:
\begin{equation}
  K_{\mathrm{a}} = 1 - \Lambda\,r^2\,,\qquad
  K_{\mathrm{f}} = 1 - 2\Lambda\,r^2\,.
\end{equation}
The cosmological constant shifts the self-consistency target
from $K=1$ to $K = 1 - O(\Lambda r^2)$, with $K_{\mathrm{f}}$ and
$K_{\mathrm{a}}$ shifted by different amounts.
$\Lambda$ is not derived from the framework; it is located.

\section{Toward general metrics}
\label{sec:general}
%===================================================================

For a general metric the proof strategy relies on the following
algebraic fact.

\begin{proposition}[Polarization identity]\label{prop:polar}
Let $R_{\mu\nu}$ be a symmetric tensor in $n$-dimensional
Lorentzian spacetime with $R = g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu} = 0$.
If $R_{\mu\nu}\,v^\mu v^\nu = 0$ for every null vector $v^\mu$,
then $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In an orthonormal frame, null vectors take the form
$v = e_0 + n^i e_i$ with $\delta_{ij}n^i n^j = 1$.
Setting $n = \pm e_k$ yields $R_{0k}=0$ and $R_{00}+R_{kk}=0$
for each $k$.
Setting $n = (e_i + e_j)/\sqrt{2}$ then gives $R_{ij}=0$ for $i\neq j$.
Thus $R_{\mu\nu} = R_{00}\,\mathrm{diag}(-1,-1,\ldots,-1)$.
The trace condition $R = -R_{00} + (n{-}1)(-R_{00}) = -nR_{00} = 0$
forces $R_{00}=0$, hence $R_{\mu\nu}=0$.
\end{proof}

If one can show that $K_{\mathrm{f}}(v)=1$ on each null 2-plane
implies $R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu = 0$---which in the spherically
symmetric case follows from the explicit computation---then the
polarization identity combined with $R=0$ (from $K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$)
yields $R_{\mu\nu}=0$ for any metric.

This step requires the Gauss--Codazzi relations for null
hypersurfaces; it parallels the logical structure of Jacobson's
original argument (where Clausius plays the role of $K{=}1$).
The general proof is therefore \emph{incomplete} and is deferred
to future work.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%===================================================================

\subsection{What is new}

\begin{enumerate}
\item The field-level observables $K_{\mathrm{f}}$ and $K_{\mathrm{a}}$,
  giving $R=0$ and $R_{\theta\theta}=0$ a transparent cost interpretation.
\item The exact equivalence (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}), proved in
  both directions for the spherically symmetric sector.
\item The ODE $f + 2rf' + \frac{r^2}{2}f'' = 1$ and its closed-form
  general solution~\eqref{eq:gensol}.
\item The one-line derivation of the Newtonian potential from
  $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:lin}).
\item The cost reading of matter:
  $\rho = (1-K_{\mathrm{a}})/(8\pi r^2)$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What is not new}

The mathematics is standard differential geometry applied to a
well-known metric ansatz.
The solutions---Schwarzschild, Newton, Birkhoff---are
textbook results.
The matter interpretation~\eqref{eq:rho} uses Einstein's equations
as input; it is a reformulation, not an independent prediction.

\subsection{Relationship to Jacobson's derivation}

The logical architecture of the general proof
(Section~\ref{sec:general}) mirrors Jacobson's:
a \emph{local} condition is applied on every null direction,
and the polarization identity assembles the full tensor equation.
What changes is the local input:
Jacobson uses the Clausius relation;
we use $K_{\mathrm{f}}=1$ (cost self-consistency).
The patching logic is the same.

Accordingly, this work is best described as a cost-based
\emph{rewriting} of the Jacobson argument with explicit
observables, rather than an independent route to Einstein's
equations.

\subsection{Open problems}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Angular condition from cost.}
  Derive $K_{\mathrm{a}}=1$ from the cost axioms R, E, T,
  rather than imposing it by definition.
  This is the primary conceptual gap.
\item \textbf{General proof.}
  Complete the null-surface Gauss--Codazzi step to establish
  $K_{\mathrm{f}}(v)=1 \Rightarrow R_{\mu\nu}v^\mu v^\nu = 0$
  for arbitrary metrics.
\item \textbf{Singularity resolution.}
  If $\sigma_1$ has a positive lower bound $\sigma_{1,\min}>0$
  (conjectured from the conjugacy of $K$ and the boost angle),
  then curvature is bounded above by $R_{\max} \sim 2/\sigma_{1,\min}^2$,
  preventing singularity formation.
  A rough estimate gives
  $\sigma_{1,\min} \sim \ell_P/2 \approx 8\times 10^{-36}\;\mathrm{m}$,
  yielding $R_{\max} \sim 3\times 10^{70}\;\mathrm{m^{-2}}$
  (Planck curvature).
  Proving $\sigma_{1,\min}>0$ remains open (Open Question~7
  in~\cite{Li2026math}).
\item \textbf{Testable predictions.}
  In the vacuum sector the equivalence $K_{\mathrm{f}}{=}1
  \wedge K_{\mathrm{a}}{=}1 \Leftrightarrow R_{\mu\nu}{=}0$
  is exact; no deviation from GR is predicted.
  This limitation is shared by all known derivations of Einstein's
  equations~\cite{Jacobson1995,Padmanabhan2010,Verlinde2011}.
\end{enumerate}
